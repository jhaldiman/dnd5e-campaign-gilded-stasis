That sounds like a blast. Since the "blank page" is often the hardest part of world-building, it helps to start with the Big Hook—the central tension that makes the world feel alive.

Here are four distinct campaign "vibes" to get the gears turning:
1. The Shifting Labyrinth (Dungeon Crawler / Survival)

The entire world exists within an impossibly large, ever-changing megastructure. Civilizations live in "rooms" the size of countries, but every few decades, the "Shift" occurs—walls move, climates change, and old maps become useless.

    The Conflict: A major Shift is coming sooner than predicted, and the players are "Wayfinders" tasked with finding a stable path for their refugees.

2. After the Golden Age (High Fantasy / Mystery)

Magic used to be as common as breathing, fueled by a "Great Weave" in the sky. Then, it shattered. Now, magic is rare, volatile, and often dangerous.

    The Conflict: Ancient "relic-tech" is waking up. The players find a functional artifact from the Golden Age that shouldn't work, making them targets for every power-hungry faction in the land.

3. The Sea of Floating Isles (Exploration / Steampunk)

The ground is a toxic, impenetrable mist. Humanity lives on floating islands, traveling via airships or winged mounts. Resources are scarce, and "Sky Pirates" are more than just a campfire story.

    The Conflict: The islands are slowly losing their buoyancy and sinking into the mist. The players must find the "Anchor Keys" to keep their home afloat.

4. The Accord of Monsters (Political / Roleplay-Heavy)

The traditional "heroic" races (Humans, Elves, Dwarves) lost the Great War. The world is now ruled by a sophisticated empire of Orcs, Hobgoblins, and Tieflings. It’s a time of relative peace, but the "Good" races are now the underground rebels.

    The Conflict: The players are agents of the Monster Empire (or rebels within it) dealing with a brewing civil war between the "Civilized Monsters" and those who want to return to the old ways of chaos.

This is a great framework. Starting at level 3 or 5 is the "sweet spot"—it skips the fragile "one-shot by a goblin" phase and lets the players feel like the established heroes you want them to be.

Since you prefer a lighthearted, heroic tone with political intrigue, a "Great Tournament" or "Gilded Age" setting works perfectly. It allows for bright colors and heroism while masking the darker schemes of a BBEG behind bureaucracy and ballrooms.

Here is a BBEG-centric concept designed for a Level 3–15 arc:
The BBEG: Archduke Valerius "The Philanthropist"

The Public Face: A charismatic, beloved noble who has funded every orphanage, bridge, and festival in the Tri-Kingdoms for a decade. He is the ultimate "Good Guy" politician.
The Secret Goal: Valerius believes the mortal realms are too chaotic to survive the next planar alignment. His goal is "The Gilded Stasis"—a massive ritual that will effectively "pause" time in the kingdom, preserving it in a perfect, eternal, unchanging golden age.
The Twist: To power this, he needs to "harvest" the heroic potential (the literal XP/Soul-energy) of the realm’s greatest champions.
The BBEG’s Iterative Plan

You can track the campaign's progress by what Valerius is doing to advance this goal:

    Phase 1: The Recruitment (Levels 3–5)

        Goal: Identify and "verify" rising heroes.

        The Intersection: Valerius sponsors a Grand Heroic Games or a "Monster Clearing Initiative." The players are hired as his "Golden Squad."

        Intrigue: They start noticing that rival adventuring parties who "retire" under Valerius’s care are never seen again, or return as strangely hollow, complacent versions of themselves.

    Phase 2: The Material Components (Levels 6–10)

        Goal: Secure the "Anchors of Reality" needed for the Stasis ritual.

        The Intersection: Valerius sends the players on heroic-sounding quests to "recover stolen artifacts" from "dangerous cults" (who are actually historians trying to stop him).

        The Shift: The players begin to see the cracks. The "villains" they are fighting are actually trying to warn them. Political tension rises as Valerius starts absorbing the powers of the neighboring monarchs "for their own protection."

    Phase 3: The Gilded Stasis (Levels 11–15)

        Goal: Activate the ritual during a massive, kingdom-wide celebration.

        The Intersection: The players are now the only ones powerful enough to stop him, but because of his political maneuvering, they are branded as "Traitors to Paradise."

        The Finale: A high-stakes confrontation in a palace made of literal solidified time, where they must decide if a "perfect, safe world" is worth the loss of free will.

Why this fits your style:

    Tone: It stays lighthearted because the world looks beautiful and the players are celebrated as celebrities for the first half.

    Intrigue: The "villain" is a friend. The players have to navigate high-society galas and political optics to expose him without becoming public enemies.

    Heroism: The players get to be the classic "Big Damn Heroes" saving the world from a fate that sounds good on paper but is horrifying in practice.

That is a classic, rewarding way to build a campaign. Starting with "low-stakes" heroics makes the transition into high-society politics feel earned. It grounds the players in the world and gives them a "home base" to care about before the Archduke starts messing with it.

To bridge the gap between Level 3/5 and the Grand Heroic Games, we can set them up in a bustling frontier province. Here’s a three-stage "Introduction Arc" that builds their reputation.
The Setup: The Silver-Leaf Province

This is a prosperous, scenic region famous for its vineyards and ancient ruins. It’s "lighthearted" but has clear problems the players can solve to gain fame.
Stage 1: The "Local Legends" Quests (Levels 3–4)

The players are already known in the town of Oakhaven. They aren't rookies, but they haven't "gone pro" yet.

    Quest A: The Clockwork Cattle. A local eccentric tinkerer’s automated harvesters have gone haywire and are "harvesting" the town’s prize-winning livestock. It’s a fun, chaotic combat/social encounter.

    Quest B: The Wine-Cellar Wight. A famous vineyard’s deepest cellar has breached an ancient, forgotten tomb. The players need to clear the undead without breaking the vintage 400-year-old bottles (adds a fun tactical challenge!).

    The Hook: After completing these, a Herald of the Archduke is seen in town, taking notes on "notable local talent."

Stage 2: The "Regional Heroes" Conflict (Level 5)

The stakes rise. A legitimate threat appears that the local guard can’t handle.

    The Quest: The Bandit Prince of Emerald Pass. A charismatic bandit leader has been intercepting trade. He’s not "evil"—he’s actually a disgraced knight.

    The Twist: If the players defeat or negotiate with him, they find a Golden Invitation in his possession. He was supposed to be a contestant in the Games but turned to crime instead.

    The Reward: The town holds a massive feast in the players' honor. During the toast, the Herald steps forward and officially invites them to the capital for the Archduke’s Invitational.

The Transition to Political Intrigue

As they travel to the capital, you can start seeding the BBEG’s goals and the political landscape:

    The Rival Party: They meet "The Iron Vanguard" on the road—another group of heroes invited to the games. They are friendly, boastful, and heroic. This makes it much more impactful later when the Archduke "preserves" them.

    The "Perfect" Border: As they get closer to the Archduke’s seat of power, the players notice the roads are too clean. The peasants are too happy. It feels like a stage play.

    The First Meeting: Upon arrival, Archduke Valerius doesn't meet them in a throne room. He meets them at a charity gala. He knows their names, their recent exploits in Oakhaven, and praises their "limitless potential."

Iterating on the BBEG's Goals

Now that we have the start, we can look at the BBEG’s immediate "Intersections":
Level	What Valerius is Doing	How it intersects with the Players
3-5	Scouting for "Pure Souls."	He sends the Herald to watch them; he "sponsors" their gear upgrades as a "gift of goodwill."
6-8	Thinning the Herd.	The Games begin. He puts the players in scenarios that look like "heroic tests" but are designed to see how much "heroic energy" they generate under pressure.
9+	The "Retirement" Plan.	He begins offering the players land, titles, and "eternal rest" in a beautiful estate—which is actually the first prototype of his Stasis Field.

This is shaping up to be a fantastic campaign structure. By starting with "grounded" heroics and transitioning into "high-society" peril, you create a powerful contrast that makes the BBEG’s ultimate plan feel even more unsettling.

Here is a detailed overview of the campaign—from the BBEG’s secret mechanics to the players' path from local legends to planar saviors.
I. The BBEG: Archduke Valerius "The Philanthropist"

    Public Persona: A charismatic, soft-spoken visionary who has brought unprecedented peace and prosperity to the Tri-Kingdoms. He doesn't wear armor; he wears fine silks and a warm smile.

    The Hidden Motive: Valerius is a "Tragic Perfectionist." He believes that all suffering stems from change and time. His goal is to cast "The Gilded Stasis," a 9th-level chronomancy ritual that will lock the entire realm into a beautiful, unchanging "Forever Noon."

    The Resource: To fuel a spell of this magnitude, he requires "Heroic Essence"—the literal soul-energy generated by people who overcome great odds. He isn't killing heroes; he’s "preserving" them in magical crystals to act as batteries for his eternal utopia.

II. Campaign Tone & Pillars

    Tone: Lighthearted, vibrant, and heroic. The world is beautiful and worth saving. The "darkness" is subtle—creeping dread rather than gore and decay.

    Primary Pillars: * Social/Intrigue: Navigating high-society galas, deciphering the "perfect" facade of the capital, and managing public reputation.

        Combat: "Arena-style" heroic challenges and, later, high-stakes battles against the Archduke’s "Gilded Sentinels" (constructs made of gold and glass).

III. The Story Arc: From Oakhaven to the Citadel of Time
Phase 1: Local Legends (Levels 3–5)

    Location: The Silver-Leaf Province (specifically the town of Oakhaven).

    The "Run of the Mill" Quests:

        Level 3: Dealing with "The Clockwork Cattle" (haywire farming constructs) and "The Wine-Cellar Wight" (protecting vintage goods from ancient undead).

        Level 4: Clearing a "Monster Infestation" in the nearby Whispering Woods—actually a group of displaced creatures fleeing the Archduke's "urban renewal" projects.

    The Turning Point (Level 5): The players defeat the Bandit Prince of Emerald Pass. In his loot, they find a Golden Invitation. Shortly after, Herald Elara, a polished and polite envoy, arrives to officially scout the players for the Archduke’s Invitational.

Phase 2: The Grand Heroic Games (Levels 6–10)

    Location: The Capital City of Aurelius, a city that literally shines with magical upkeep.

    The "Games": A series of spectacular, televised-style events (The Labyrinth of Trials, The Sky-Ship Regatta, The Mock Siege).

    The Intrigue:

        The players meet other "Contestant Parties." One by one, winning parties are "retired" to private villas and never seen again.

        The players are gifted a luxurious estate, but they find it is heavily surveilled by "Unseen Servants."

        They are approached by The Silver Crows, a small resistance group of disgraced nobles who claim the Archduke is stealing the "spark" from the world.

Phase 3: The Gilded Stasis (Levels 11–15)

    The Reveal: The players discover the "Hall of Statues"—the previous winners of the games, frozen in time, their energy being siphoned.

    The Conflict: The Archduke begins the final countdown to the ritual. The players must navigate the political fallout of being branded "terrorists" for opposing the beloved Archduke.

    The Climax: A final assault on the Citadel of Chronos during the "Festival of Eternal Light." They must break the Stasis Field before the sun stops moving forever.

IV. Iterative BBEG Design
